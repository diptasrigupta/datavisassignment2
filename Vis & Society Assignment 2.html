Discoveries & Insights

Summary

In conclusion, there is reasonable evidence to suggest there is a correlation between the amount of land reserved for single-family housing and higher incomes. Neighborhoods that are exclusively single-family-zoned are much more likely to have houshold incomes over 200,000 dollars. These high-income, highly-zoned neighborhoods do not seem to be overtly clustered in a single area. Single-family neighborhoods are also likely to have a significantly higher proportion of White residents, while municipalities with less exclusionary housing policies tend to be more diverse. Single-family neighborhoods also have a lower proportion of young adults, in favor of older adults with children or teenagers. All these point to zoning being somewhat exclusionary, favoring higher-income White families. Interestingly, the percentage of land being zoned for single-family homes does not seem to affect population density that much. This analysis suggests that change policies regarding single-family zoning may affect the demographics and income of neighborhoods.
